Question 01

What topic will you regret not having addressed in 90 days?

Companies generally don't have a vision problem. They have a prioritization problem.

The most costly topics are rarely those we don't see. They're often the ones we keep postponing.

Every executive committee has its list of important topics. They are known. They are documented. They are regularly brought up. Yet some remain in limbo for months — not because they lack importance, but because they never seem urgent enough.

Topics that always wait their turn

  • Critical knowledge concentrated in a few minds
  • Information that exists but remains difficult to use
  • A decision slowed by lack of visibility
  • A process accepted even though it no longer satisfies anyone
When an important topic stays too long in the "later" category, it often ends up in the "too late" category.

Artificial Intelligence becomes valuable when it finally makes it possible to solve problems the company already knew about, but had never managed to address simply.

It doesn't bring knowledge. It makes it possible to leverage what already exists.

Question 02

What would your best managers do
if you finally gave them back 20% of their time?

Companies often talk about productivity. Leaders should perhaps talk more about leverage.

Not all hours have the same value. An hour saved on an administrative task doesn't have the same impact as an hour given back to a manager capable of accelerating a project, developing a key talent, unblocking a decision or advancing a strategic client.

The issue isn't the time gained. The issue is what finally becomes possible.

In many companies, the most experienced managers are also the most in demand. They arbitrate, unblock, explain, reassure — and sometimes compensate for the limits of systems or the organization. This is often a sign that they create value. It is sometimes a sign that they absorb too much of it.

If tomorrow they got back 20% of their time, what would you build?

  • Better execution of strategic priorities
  • A stronger management pipeline
  • A stronger and more durable client relationship
  • Faster growth in key markets
  • Better-informed decisions, made earlier
The scarcest resource isn't time. It's the attention of people capable of moving the company forward.

Artificial Intelligence becomes valuable when it makes it possible to free up this resource. Not to do more. To do better.

Executive question

What could your company achieve if your best managers spent more time building than compensating?

Question 03

What will remain when 20 years of experience retire?

Every company has an org chart. Yet some rely on a few people far more than on their org chart.

When a company depends too much on a few people, it becomes more fragile than it thinks.

Some expertise is visible. Other expertise is nearly invisible: why a strategic client behaves in a certain way, which risks to monitor before they appear in the indicators, which decisions have already been tested and why some succeeded. This knowledge doesn't appear in any org chart, any process, any dashboard.

Signals not to normalize

  • Some decisions only move forward when a few specific people are involved
  • Some topics slow down as soon as an expert is absent
  • Some knowledge is known but difficult to transfer
  • Some answers exist without anyone really knowing where to find them
The risk isn't a person leaving. The risk is discovering, after they leave, everything only they knew.

Artificial Intelligence now makes it possible to structure, retrieve and share this experience before it leaves the company. The challenge isn't to preserve documents. The challenge is to reduce dependence on a few people to secure the collective capacity to decide and act.

Executive question

Which decisions in your company still rely more on certain individuals than on the organization itself?

Question 04

Which client do you believe is loyal when they may no longer be?

Companies invest a great deal of energy in acquiring new clients. They sometimes invest less attention in those they believe they've already won.

The greatest risk isn't always losing a client. The greatest risk is not seeing that they're drifting away.

Few clients announce their departure in advance. They order a little less, respond a little later, consult other players, ask fewer questions, share less information. The relationship doesn't deteriorate suddenly. It weakens gradually.

Signals not to normalize

  • Some long-standing clients are becoming quieter
  • Some recurring opportunities disappear without explanation
  • Some exchanges become more transactional, less strategic
  • Some decisions take longer than before
A client's loyalty is rarely measured by what they bought yesterday. It's measured by what they'll choose tomorrow.

Artificial Intelligence becomes valuable when it helps reveal shifts that no one was seeing individually, but which become evident once connected. The challenge isn't to sell more. The challenge is to understand earlier what is genuinely changing with your clients.

Executive question

Which clients do you still consider loyal when their behavior may already be telling a different story?

Question 05

Which blind spot is already costing
more than your biggest investment?

Leaders decide with the information they have. The problem is never what we know poorly. It's what we don't yet know we're ignoring.

A blind spot doesn't cost at the moment of discovery. It has been costing since the beginning — in silence.

In every organization, some areas remain systematically out of sight: a market evolving faster than perceived, a competitor advancing without their signals being flagged, an internal trend establishing itself before it's named. These blind spots don't lack data. They often lack perspective, connections between sources, or someone to flag them at the right level.

The most costly blind spots are rarely the most visible

  • A market or segment evolving faster than your internal sensors
  • A dependency whose size you only measure when it becomes critical
  • A strategic decision made on partial or outdated data
  • A weak signal each person perceives individually, but that no one has connected
It's not information that's missing. It's the ability to see it before it costs.

Artificial Intelligence becomes valuable when it connects data that no one was linking, and reveals what the organization didn't know it wasn't seeing. The challenge isn't to monitor everything. The challenge is to see earlier what will matter.

Executive question

Which topic is currently moving through your organization without anyone truly responsible for monitoring it?
